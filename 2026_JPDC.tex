	% ── Abstract ──────────────────────────────────────────────
	\begin{abstract}
		Federated distillation (FD) enables collaborative edge learning by
		exchanging soft predictions rather than model parameters, offering
		communication efficiency and architectural flexibility.  However,
		deploying FD over heterogeneous edge networks faces a critical yet
		overlooked challenge: device stragglers whose unpredictable
		latencies dominate synchronous round times and degrade system
		throughput.  Existing asynchronous federated learning methods target
		parameter-level aggregation and cannot be directly applied to
		logit-based distillation, where partial uploads and quality-aware
		scheduling introduce unique design constraints.  This paper presents
		DASH (Deadline-Aware Straggler-Handling), an asynchronous
		distillation scheduling framework with straggler tolerance.  We
		design a three-outcome straggler model (complete, partial, timeout)
		driven by log-normal latency distributions, and integrate device
		completion probabilities into a submodular greedy selection
		algorithm.  We prove that the resulting expected quality retains a
		constant-factor approximation ratio relative to the deterministic
		optimum, with degradation bounded linearly by the worst-case
		straggler probability.  Three timeout policies (fixed, adaptive,
		and partial-accept) provide system-level trade-offs between round
		latency and knowledge utilization, with a deadline floor mechanism
		that prevents adaptive deadline collapse.  Experiments on CIFAR-100
		and EMNIST-ByClass (Extended MNIST) with up to 200 devices
		demonstrate that DASH achieves accuracy comparable to synchronous
		scheduling while reducing wall-clock convergence time by
		$2$--$3.5\times$, with the speedup increasing monotonically as the
		device pool scales.
	\end{abstract}

	\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
	% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
	
	The proliferation of IoT devices at the network edge has created
	enormous volumes of data that are too costly or too sensitive to
	transmit to a centralized cloud~\citep{shi_edge_computing}.
	Federated learning~(FL) addresses this challenge by training models
	collaboratively across distributed devices while keeping raw data
	local~\citep{mcmahan_fedavg_2017, kairouz_fl_advances_2021}.  As
	the scale of edge deployments grows---projections suggest tens of
	billions of connected devices within the next few
	years~\citep{lim_fl_mobile_edge_2020}---the heterogeneity of
	participating devices has become a defining obstacle.  Devices differ
	not only in data distributions and model
	capacities~\citep{qin_kdfl_survey_2025}, but more fundamentally in
	their \emph{latency profiles}: CPU frequency, network bandwidth,
	background load, and transient interference all contribute to
	round-trip times that can vary by orders of magnitude across devices
	and across rounds.
	
	In a synchronous FL protocol, the server waits for every selected
	device to report before advancing to the next round.  The round time
	is therefore determined by the slowest participant---the
	\emph{straggler}.  As the device pool grows, the probability that at
	least one device experiences extreme delay increases rapidly, and the
	synchronous round time can inflate by a factor of three to five
	compared to the median device
	latency~\citep{nguyen_fedbuff_2022}.  This straggler bottleneck is
	particularly acute in edge environments, where network
	variability and resource contention introduce heavy-tailed latency
	distributions that are difficult to predict or
	control~\citep{mora_kdfl_guide_2024}.
	
	Federated distillation~(FD) has emerged as a communication-efficient
	alternative to parameter-based FL by exchanging soft predictions
	(logits) rather than model
	weights~\citep{li_fedmd_2019, lin_feddf_2020}.  Because logits are
	compact and model-architecture-agnostic, FD naturally supports
	heterogeneous device populations and reduces per-round communication
	by orders of magnitude~\citep{hinton_kd_2015}.  Recent advances have
	improved the quality, robustness, and security of the distilled
	knowledge through selective transmission~\citep{gad_fedskd_2024},
	adversarial-guided boundary
	attention~\citep{wu_feddfa_2024}, uncertainty-driven feature
	distillation~\citep{shao_fedufd_2025}, and secure logit
	aggregation~\citep{chen_svafd_2025}.  However, virtually all existing
	FD methods assume synchronous operation: they take device
	participation as given and do not address what happens when a device
	fails to deliver its logits within the round.
	
	A natural response to the straggler problem is asynchronous
	aggregation.  In parameter-based FL, methods such as
	FedAsync~\citep{xie_async_fedopt_2019} and
	FedBuff~\citep{nguyen_fedbuff_2022} allow the server to incorporate
	device updates as they arrive, tolerating staleness at the cost of
	potential model drift.  More recently, knowledge distillation has been
	integrated into asynchronous FL to mitigate the effects of stale
	updates: FedEcho~\citep{wang_fedecho_2025} uses uncertainty-aware
	distillation to down-weight unreliable predictions from delayed
	clients, and FedTd~\citep{xu_fedtd_2025} transfers straggler
	knowledge through a two-stage fusion scheme.  These methods
	demonstrate the value of combining distillation with asynchronous
	operation, but they are designed for parameter-level aggregation and
	do not exploit the structural properties unique to logit-based
	communication.  In particular, FD admits a richer set of straggler
	outcomes than parameter FL: a device may complete local training but
	only partially upload its logit vectors before a deadline expires,
	yielding a \emph{partial contribution} whose value is proportional to
	the number of vectors received.  This partial-upload possibility has
	no counterpart in parameter-based FL, where a partially transmitted
	weight vector is not directly usable.
	
	On the scheduling side, principled device selection has been studied
	extensively for parameter-based
	FL~\citep{nishio_fedcs_2019, lai_oort_2021, balakrishnan_divfl_2022},
	with submodular optimization providing provable approximation
	guarantees~\citep{zhang_submod_tosn_2024}.  However, these methods
	optimize for gradient diversity or convergence speed rather than for
	the saturation quality model that characterizes logit-based
	distillation, and none of them incorporate device \emph{completion
		probability} into the selection objective.  The gap is clear:
	\emph{no existing framework jointly addresses asynchronous scheduling,
		straggler tolerance, and quality-aware resource allocation for
		federated distillation.}
	
	This paper presents DASH (Deadline-Aware Straggler-Handling), an
	asynchronous scheduling framework designed specifically for federated
	distillation over heterogeneous edge networks.  Rather than waiting
	for all selected devices, DASH sets a per-round deadline and collects
	whatever logits arrive---complete or partial---before the deadline
	expires.  The scheduling decision itself is straggler-aware: device
	completion probabilities, derived from a log-normal latency model,
	are integrated into a submodular greedy selection algorithm that
	jointly optimizes for knowledge quality, communication cost, and
	reliability.  Three timeout policies---fixed, adaptive, and
	partial-accept---provide system-level knobs for trading off round
	latency against knowledge utilization.  A deadline floor mechanism
	prevents the adaptive policy from collapsing into a self-reinforcing
	spiral.  The framework is agnostic to the underlying distillation
	algorithm: it wraps any FD aggregation mechanism with a deployment
	layer that handles device selection, volume allocation, and straggler
	management.
	
	The main contributions of this paper are as follows.
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item We design an asynchronous federated distillation protocol with
		a three-outcome straggler model (complete, partial, timeout) driven
		by log-normal latency distributions.  Unlike the binary
		delivered-or-not model of parameter-based FL, the partial outcome
		exploits the independence of individual logit vectors to retain useful
		knowledge from interrupted uploads (Section~\ref{sec:system}).
		
		\item We introduce straggler-aware device selection by embedding
		completion probabilities into the submodular quality function via the
		effective fidelity $\tilde{\rho}_i = \pi_i(D) \cdot \rho_i$.  We
		prove that the modified quality function preserves submodularity
		(Proposition~\ref{prop:sub_async}), and derive an expected quality
		bound of
		$(1-\bar{p})\tfrac{1}{2}(1-1/e) \cdot Q^*$, where $\bar{p}$ is the
		worst-case straggler probability and $Q^*$ is the deterministic
		optimum (Theorem~\ref{thm:straggler_bound},
		Section~\ref{sec:sa_select}).
		
		\item We propose three timeout policies---fixed, adaptive, and
		partial-accept---with a $D_{\min}$ floor mechanism that prevents
		deadline spiral under the adaptive policy
		(Section~\ref{sec:timeout}).
		
		\item We conduct comprehensive experiments on CIFAR-100 and
		EMNIST-ByClass with up to 200 devices, demonstrating that DASH
		achieves accuracy comparable to synchronous scheduling while reducing
		wall-clock convergence time by $2$--$3.5\times$.  The speedup
		increases monotonically with pool size, reaching $3.49\times$ at
		$M = 200$, and DASH maintains stable accuracy across a wide range of
		straggler severities and privacy levels
		(Section~\ref{sec:eval}).
	\end{enumerate}
	
	The remainder of this paper is organized as follows.
	Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work on federated

	Section~\ref{sec:system} presents the system architecture and

	% --------------------------------------------------
	% 2.1  Federated Distillation Algorithms
	% --------------------------------------------------
	\subsection{Federated distillation algorithms}\label{sec:rw_fd}
	
	Federated distillation (FD) replaces the exchange of model parameters
	with the exchange of soft predictions (logits) computed on a shared
	public dataset, enabling communication-efficient and
	architecture-heterogeneous collaborative learning.
	FedMD~\citep{li_fedmd_2019} introduced this paradigm by having
	heterogeneous local models align their outputs on public data through
	knowledge distillation. FedDF~\citep{lin_feddf_2020} extended the idea
	to ensemble distillation, where the server fuses logits from all
	participants into a single global model without requiring shared
	model architectures. FedGKT~\citep{he_fedgkt_2020} pushed this
	further toward edge deployment by training small networks on devices
	and transferring knowledge to a larger server model, explicitly
	targeting computation-constrained scenarios.
	
	Subsequent work has focused on improving the quality and robustness
	of the distilled knowledge. \citet{gad_fedskd_2024}
	proposed selective knowledge distillation over bandwidth-constrained
	wireless networks, transmitting only a filtered subset of logits to
	reduce communication cost while preserving accuracy.
	Fed-DFA~\citep{wu_feddfa_2024} addressed the capacity gap between
	heterogeneous models by leveraging adversarial examples to guide
	distillation toward samples near decision boundaries, where logit
	distributions carry the most informative knowledge.
	FedUFD~\citep{shao_fedufd_2025} introduced uncertainty-driven feature
	distillation for personalized edge computing, exploiting prediction
	uncertainty to improve the global-to-local knowledge transfer.
	SVAFD~\citep{chen_svafd_2025} designed the first secure aggregation
	protocol specifically for FD, incorporating quality-aware logit
	filtering to defend against poisoning attacks while remaining
	resilient to stragglers and colluding clients.
	Selective-FD~\citep{shao_selective_fd_2023} proposed a selective
	knowledge-sharing mechanism that identifies and filters ambiguous
	logits before aggregation, improving both accuracy and data protection
	by avoiding the transfer of low-confidence predictions.
	FedVKD~\citep{he_fedvkd_2026} introduced an adaptive virtual
	data-based knowledge distillation framework that generates synthetic
	reference samples on the server side, eliminating the dependency on a
	pre-existing public dataset while maintaining competitive distillation
	quality across heterogeneous device architectures.
	
	These methods collectively advance what knowledge to distill and how
	to distill it, yet they uniformly treat device participation as
	given: every selected device is assumed to complete its logit
	computation and upload within the round. None of the above works
	addresses the scheduling question of \emph{whom to select} and
	\emph{how much to allocate} under resource constraints, nor do they
	model the possibility that a device may only partially deliver its
	logits due to latency variations. DASH complements this body of work
	by operating at the deployment layer: it takes any FD aggregation
	mechanism as a black box and wraps it with a deadline-aware scheduling
	framework that determines device participation, upload volumes, and
	straggler handling.

	% 2.2  Asynchronous FL and Straggler Tolerance
	% --------------------------------------------------
	\subsection{Asynchronous federated learning and straggler tolerance}\label{sec:rw_async}
	
	In synchronous federated learning, the server waits for all selected
	devices before aggregation, making each round as slow as the slowest
	participant. This straggler bottleneck has motivated a rich line of
	asynchronous methods. \citet{xie_async_fedopt_2019}
	proposed asynchronous federated optimization that allows the server to
	incorporate device updates as they arrive, tolerating arbitrary delays
	at the cost of potential model staleness.
	FedBuff~\citep{nguyen_fedbuff_2022} introduced buffered asynchronous
	aggregation, accumulating a configurable number of updates before
	triggering a global step, which balances staleness against throughput.
	
	Recent work has begun to integrate knowledge distillation into
	asynchronous FL as a means of mitigating the harmful effects of stale
	updates. FedEcho~\citep{wang_fedecho_2025} performs server-side
	distillation using ensemble logits from both fresh and stale client
	models, employing uncertainty estimation to down-weight unreliable
	predictions from severely delayed clients.
	FedTd~\citep{xu_fedtd_2025} proposed a two-stage model fusion scheme
	that transfers knowledge from straggler models to the global model
	via distillation without delaying the training pipeline, achieving
	up to $3.3\times$ speedup under high data heterogeneity. These
	methods demonstrate that distillation can serve as a soft mechanism
	for incorporating delayed information without directly merging
	outdated parameters. In the split federated learning
	setting, MU-SplitFed~\citep{li_mu_splitfed_2025} decouples server and
	client training progress through unbalanced local updates, achieving
	linear speedup with respect to the server iteration count
	independently of straggler delays. MFL-AKD~\citep{tang_mfl_akd_2024}
	targets multimodal FL and proposes a fast knowledge transfer mechanism
	that pre-distills a pretrained vision-language model to local models,
	reducing the convergence gap caused by stragglers in cross-modal
	settings. \citet{wang_faga2_2025} proposed a
	semi-asynchronous framework that jointly considers update staleness
	and data quality in client selection, aggregating optimized local
	updates with delay-aware importance weighting. In the communication
	layer, joint compression and deadline optimization has been studied
	for parameter-based FL~\citep{chen_joint_deadline_2023}, where the
	server sets a per-round deadline and devices adaptively compress their
	gradient uploads to meet the time constraint.
	
	A common thread across these works is that they operate on
	\emph{parameter-level} updates: the stale or delayed object is a model
	weight vector or gradient, and the correction mechanism (staleness
	weighting, distillation regularization, buffered aggregation) is
	designed accordingly. In logit-based FD, however, the exchanged object
	is fundamentally different---a set of soft prediction vectors on a
	reference dataset---and admits a richer set of outcomes than the
	binary delivered-or-not dichotomy of parameter FL. Specifically, a
	device may complete local training but only partially upload its logit
	vectors before the deadline expires, yielding a partial contribution
	that retains value proportional to the uploaded fraction. DASH
	exploits this FD-specific structure through a three-outcome straggler
	model (complete, partial, timeout) and integrates device completion
	probabilities directly into the scheduling objective, a design that
	has no counterpart in the parameter-based asynchronous literature.

	% 2.3  Device Selection and Resource Scheduling
	% --------------------------------------------------
	\subsection{Device selection and resource scheduling}\label{sec:rw_selection}
	
	Deciding which devices to include in each training round is a
	fundamental problem in federated learning. \citet{nishio_fedcs_2019} proposed FedCS, which estimates
	each device's resource availability and selects a subset that can
	complete training within a wall-clock deadline, trading off
	participation breadth for round efficiency.
	Oort~\citep{lai_oort_2021} refined this idea by jointly considering
	data utility and system speed, prioritizing devices that contribute
	the most statistical value per unit of training time.
	\citet{cho_biased_2022} showed that biased client selection
	based on local loss values can provably accelerate convergence over
	uniform random sampling.
	
	Submodular optimization has emerged as a principled framework for
	client selection with provable guarantees.
	DivFL~\citep{balakrishnan_divfl_2022} formulated diverse client
	selection as facility location maximization in gradient space,
	achieving a constant-factor approximation ratio under cardinality
	constraints. \citet{zhang_submod_tosn_2024} extended
	submodular selection to jointly account for system and statistical
	heterogeneity under knapsack constraints, demonstrating improved
	time-to-accuracy in heterogeneous edge environments.
	More recent work has adapted selection strategies to emerging
	deployment scenarios: \citet{alblewi_fedscs_2025}
	applied energy-aware selection to orbital edge computing, where
	satellite devices are chosen based on predicted battery and CPU
	availability, and \citet{talukder_fedsrc_2025}
	empowered devices with self-regulation capabilities, allowing them to
	autonomously assess their data quality and abstain from rounds where
	their contribution would be detrimental. On the scheduling side,
	\citet{khan_voi_scheduling_2024} combined Age of Update with
	Data Shapley metrics to design a scheduling policy that captures both
	the freshness and value of device updates under unreliable network conditions.
	
	These selection and scheduling methods provide elegant theoretical
	frameworks, but they are uniformly designed for parameter-based FL.
	Their objective functions---gradient diversity, local loss, or update
	staleness---do not capture the saturation quality model that
	characterizes logit-based distillation, where each device's
	contribution exhibits diminishing returns governed by a
	Michaelis--Menten function and is attenuated by its privacy posture.
	Moreover, none of the existing selection methods incorporate device
	\emph{completion probability} into the optimization objective; they
	assume that a selected device either participates fully or is excluded
	entirely. DASH bridges this gap by defining a straggler-aware
	efficiency index $\tilde{\eta}_i = \pi_i(D) \cdot \rho_i / (b_i
	\theta_i)$ that jointly captures reliability, knowledge quality, and
	cost. This index preserves the submodularity of the aggregate quality
	function (Proposition~2), enabling a greedy selection algorithm with a
	provable $(1-\bar{p})\frac{1}{2}(1-1/e)$ approximation ratio relative
	to the deterministic optimum (Theorem~2).

	\section{System Architecture}\label{sec:system}
	% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
	
	We consider an edge intelligence platform in which a single edge
	server~(ES) coordinates federated distillation across a pool
	$\Mset = \{1, \dots, M\}$ of heterogeneous IoT devices connected
	through unreliable network links.  Rather than exchanging model
	parameters, the ES operates a knowledge distillation service pipeline:
	in each round, a selected subset of devices compute soft predictions
	(logits) on a shared reference dataset and upload them to the ES,
	which distills the collected knowledge into a global model.  The
	central challenge addressed in this paper is that device latencies are
	heterogeneous and stochastic, so that synchronous protocols---which
	wait for all selected devices---suffer from worst-case round times
	that grow with the pool size~$M$.
	
	% ── 3.1 System Overview ──────────────────────────────────
	\subsection{System overview}\label{sec:overview}
	
	The DASH architecture comprises three server-side modules and a
	device-side execution pipeline, as illustrated in
	Fig.~\ref{fig:system}.
	
	\begin{figure}[t]
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figs/Pipeline_Flow.png}
		\caption{DASH system architecture with three server-side modules
			and three possible device outcomes (complete, partial, timeout).}
		\label{fig:system}
	\end{figure}
	
	On the server side, the DASH Scheduler determines at the beginning of
	each round which devices to engage and how many logit vectors to
	request from each, subject to a per-round resource budget~$B$.  The
	Async Controller sets a per-round deadline~$D^{(t)}$, dispatches
	upload quotas to selected devices, and collects responses as they
	arrive; unlike synchronous protocols that block until all devices
	respond, the Async Controller closes the collection window at
	$D^{(t)}$ regardless of how many devices have reported.  The
	Aggregation Engine receives the collected logits, computes
	quality-weighted averages on the reference dataset~$\Dref$, and
	updates the global model through knowledge distillation.
	
	On the device side, upon receiving the global model
	$\mathbf{w}^{(t-1)}$ and its upload quota~$v_i^*$, each selected
	device~$i$ fine-tunes its local model on its private
	dataset~$\Dpriv$, computes logits on~$\Dref$ and applies local
	perturbation, and uploads up to~$v_i^*$ perturbed logit vectors to
	the ES\@.  The total time for this pipeline depends on the device's
	computational capability, network conditions, and random disturbances,
	all of which vary across devices and rounds.
	
	Each service round proceeds through four phases.  In the
	\emph{scheduling} phase~(P1), the DASH Scheduler computes the
	selected set~$\Sset^{(t)}$ and allocations~$\{v_i^*\}$,
	incorporating straggler probabilities
	(Section~\ref{sec:sa_select}).  In the \emph{dispatch} phase~(P2),
	the ES dispatches $(\mathbf{w}^{(t-1)}, v_i^*)$ to each
	$i \in \Sset^{(t)}$.  In the \emph{collection} phase~(P3), devices
	execute the local pipeline and upload logits while the Async
	Controller enforces deadline~$D^{(t)}$; devices that have not
	finished are either partially collected or timed out
	(Section~\ref{sec:straggler}).  Finally, in the \emph{aggregation}
	phase~(P4), the Aggregation Engine computes quality-weighted logit
	averages over the received contributions and distills them into
	$\mathbf{w}^{(t)}$ (Section~\ref{sec:aggregation}).
	
	% ── 3.2 Device Cost and Quality Model ────────────────────
	\subsection{Device cost and quality model}\label{sec:cost_quality}
	
	Each device~$i$ is characterized by a profile
	$(\rho_i, \theta_i, b_i, a_i)$.  The marginal resource cost for
	uploading one logit vector at round~$t$ is
	\begin{equation}\label{eq:cost}
		b_i^{(t)} \triangleq \omega_i^{(t)} + \mu_i,
	\end{equation}
	where $\omega_i^{(t)} > 0$ is the per-vector communication cost
	(determined by the device's network bandwidth and link quality)
	and $\mu_i > 0$ is the per-vector computation cost.  The
	activation cost $a_i > 0$ captures the fixed overhead of local model
	training; the total cost for uploading~$v_i$ vectors is thus
	$C_i(v_i) = a_i \cdot \mathbf{1}_{\{v_i > 0\}} + b_i \, v_i$.
	
	The effective quality of device~$i$'s contribution is modeled by a
	saturation function:
	\begin{equation}\label{eq:quality}
		q_i(v_i) = \rho_i \cdot \frac{v_i}{v_i + \theta_i},
	\end{equation}
	where $\theta_i > 0$ is the half-saturation constant (the volume at
	which device~$i$ attains quality $\rho_i / 2$) and
	$\rho_i \in (0, 1]$ is the privacy degradation factor quantifying
	the fraction of useful information retained after local perturbation
	($\rho_i = 1$: no perturbation; $\rho_i \to 0$: strong privacy).
	This Michaelis--Menten form captures diminishing returns
	($\partial q_i / \partial v_i > 0$,
	$\partial^2 q_i / \partial v_i^2 < 0$), bounded capacity
	($q_i \to \rho_i$), and privacy attenuation (the ceiling scales
	with~$\rho_i$).
	
	Under a per-round budget $B > 0$, the ES jointly selects a device
	subset $\Sset \subseteq \Mset$ and determines upload volumes
	$\{v_i\}_{i \in \Sset}$ to maximize aggregate quality:
	\begin{subequations}\label{eq:det_problem}
		\begin{align}
			\max_{\Sset,\,\{v_i\}} \;\;
			& \sum_{i \in \Sset} \rho_i \,\frac{v_i}{v_i + \theta_i}
			\label{eq:det_obj} \\
			\text{s.t.} \;\;
			& \sum_{i \in \Sset}(a_i + b_i \, v_i) \leq B,
			\label{eq:det_budget} \\
			& v_i \in [0, \vmax],\; \forall\, i \in \Sset,
			\;\; \Sset \subseteq \Mset.
			\label{eq:det_bound}
		\end{align}
	\end{subequations}
	Problem~\eqref{eq:det_problem} is a mixed-integer program that is
	NP-hard in general, but admits efficient approximation through the
	structural properties exploited in Section~\ref{sec:scheduling}.
	
	% ── 3.3 Straggler Latency Model ─────────────────────────
	\subsection{Straggler latency model}\label{sec:straggler}
	
	In a synchronous protocol, the ES waits for all selected devices to
	finish before aggregation; the round time is therefore dominated by
	the slowest device.  As~$M$ grows, the probability of at least one
	extreme straggler increases rapidly, making synchronous operation
	increasingly impractical.
	
	We model the round-trip latency of device~$i$ as the sum of three
	components:
	\begin{equation}\label{eq:latency}
		\tau_i = \tau_i^{\mathrm{comp}} + \tau_i^{\mathrm{comm}}
		+ \tau_i^{\mathrm{noise}},
	\end{equation}
	where
	$\tau_i^{\mathrm{comp}} = c_i \cdot |\Dpriv| \cdot E$ is the local
	computation time, with~$c_i$ the per-sample processing rate and~$E$
	the number of local training epochs (deterministic,
	device-dependent);
	$\tau_i^{\mathrm{comm}} = v_i^* \cdot s / r_i$ is the upload
	transmission time, with~$s$ the payload size per logit vector
	and~$r_i$ the instantaneous link rate (deterministic given the
	allocation~$v_i^*$); and
	$\tau_i^{\mathrm{noise}} \sim \mathrm{LogNormal}(\mu_n, \sigma_n)$
	captures random disturbances from queueing, OS scheduling, and
	transient interference (stochastic, i.i.d.\ across devices and
	rounds).  The log-normal model for~$\tau_i^{\mathrm{noise}}$ is
	well-established in the networking literature for representing
	heavy-tailed latency variations; the parameter~$\sigma_n$ controls
	the severity of straggler behavior.
	
	At each round~$t$, the ES sets a deadline~$D^{(t)}$
	(Section~\ref{sec:timeout}).  Each selected device~$i$ falls into
	exactly one of three outcome categories:
	
	\begin{definition}[Device Outcome]\label{def:outcome}
		Given deadline~$D^{(t)}$ and device latency~$\tau_i$, device~$i$'s
		outcome is classified as follows.
		\begin{enumerate}
			\item[(C)] Complete ($\tau_i \leq D^{(t)}$): the ES receives all
			$v_i^*$ logit vectors, i.e., $v_i^{\mathrm{recv}} = v_i^*$.
			\item[(P)] Partial
			($\tau_i^{\mathrm{comp}} \leq D^{(t)} < \tau_i$): the device has
			finished computation but its upload is interrupted; the ES receives
			\begin{equation}\label{eq:partial}
				v_i^{\mathrm{recv}} = \Bigl\lfloor v_i^*
				\cdot \frac{D^{(t)} - \tau_i^{\mathrm{comp}}}
				{\tau_i^{\mathrm{comm}}} \Bigr\rfloor
			\end{equation}
			logit vectors.
			\item[(T)] Timeout ($D^{(t)} < \tau_i^{\mathrm{comp}}$): the device
			has not completed local computation; no logits are received
			($v_i^{\mathrm{recv}} = 0$).
		\end{enumerate}
	\end{definition}
	
	This three-outcome model is specific to federated distillation: in
	parameter-based FL, a device either delivers its full model update or
	contributes nothing, since a partially transmitted weight vector is
	not directly usable.  In FD, however, each logit vector is an
	independent soft prediction on one reference sample, so a partial
	upload retains value proportional to the number of vectors received.
	The quality function~\eqref{eq:quality} naturally accommodates this
	by evaluating $q_i(v_i^{\mathrm{recv}})$ instead of $q_i(v_i^*)$
	when partial contributions are accepted.
	
	The completion probability of device~$i$ under deadline~$D$ is
	\begin{equation}\label{eq:pi}
		\pi_i(D) \triangleq \Pr[\tau_i \leq D]
		= F_{\mathrm{LN}}\!\bigl(D - \tau_i^{\mathrm{comp}}
		- \tau_i^{\mathrm{comm}};\; \mu_n, \sigma_n\bigr),
	\end{equation}
	where~$F_{\mathrm{LN}}$ is the log-normal CDF evaluated at the
	residual slack $D - \tau_i^{\mathrm{comp}} - \tau_i^{\mathrm{comm}}$
	(set to zero if the slack is non-positive).  This quantity plays a
	central role in straggler-aware scheduling
	(Section~\ref{sec:sa_select}).
	
	\begin{definition}[Straggler Rate]\label{def:straggler_rate}
		The per-round straggler rate is the fraction of selected devices that
		fail to deliver complete logits by the deadline:
		\begin{equation}\label{eq:straggler_rate}
			p^{(t)} = \frac{|\{i \in \Sset^{(t)} :
				\tau_i > D^{(t)}\}|}{|\Sset^{(t)}|}.
		\end{equation}
	\end{definition}
	
	% ── 3.4 Asynchronous Protocol ────────────────────────────
	\subsection{Asynchronous protocol}\label{sec:async_protocol}
	
	Algorithm~\ref{alg:async_kaas} formalizes the per-round operation of
	the DASH framework.  Compared to a synchronous counterpart, the
	protocol introduces three modifications: (i)~a deadline~$D^{(t)}$ is
	computed before dispatch and clamped to a minimum
	value~$D_{\min}$ (line~3), (ii)~device completion
	probabilities $\pi_i(D^{(t)})$ are incorporated into the scheduling
	decision (line~4), and (iii)~after the deadline expires, only complete
	and (optionally) partial contributions are aggregated
	(lines~9--13).  All logits are computed using the current-round
	global model~$\mathbf{w}^{(t-1)}$, so there is no model
	staleness---a deliberate design choice that keeps the aggregation
	semantics clean and avoids the need for staleness correction weights.
	
	\begin{algorithm}[t]
		\caption{DASH: Asynchronous FD Scheduling Protocol}
		\label{alg:async_kaas}
		\begin{algorithmic}[1]
			\REQUIRE Device pool $\Mset$, budget $B$, rounds $T$,
			reference dataset $\Dref$, timeout policy $\mathcal{P}$,
			deadline floor $D_{\min}$
			\ENSURE Global model $\mathbf{w}^{(T)}$
			\STATE Initialize $\mathbf{w}^{(0)}$;\;
			$\mathcal{H} \gets \emptyset$
			\COMMENT{latency history}
			\FOR{$t = 1$ \TO $T$}
			\STATE $D^{(t)} \!\gets\!
			\max\bigl(D_{\min},\;
			\mathcal{P}.\mathrm{Deadline}(t, \mathcal{H})\bigr)$
			\STATE $(\Sset^{(t)}\!, \{v_i^*\}) \!\gets\!
			\mathrm{DASH\text{-}Select}(\Mset, B, D^{(t)})$
			\STATE Dispatch $(\mathbf{w}^{(t-1)}, v_i^*)$
			to each $i \in \Sset^{(t)}$
			\STATE Wait until wall-clock time reaches $D^{(t)}$
			\STATE // Collect responses
			\STATE $\Srecv \gets \emptyset$
			\FOR{each $i \in \Sset^{(t)}$}
			\IF{outcome$(i)$ = Complete}
			\STATE $\Srecv \!\gets\! \Srecv \cup \{(i, v_i^*)\}$
			\ELSIF{outcome$(i)$ = Partial
				\AND $\mathcal{P}$ accepts partial}
			\STATE $\Srecv \!\gets\! \Srecv
			\cup \{(i, v_i^{\mathrm{recv}})\}$
			\ENDIF
			\ENDFOR
			\STATE // Aggregate and distill
			\IF{$\Srecv \neq \emptyset$}
			\STATE Compute $\bar{\mathbf{z}}^{(t)}$
			via Eq.~\eqref{eq:agg_weight}
			\STATE Update $\mathbf{w}^{(t)}$
			via Eq.~\eqref{eq:kl}
			\ELSE
			\STATE $\mathbf{w}^{(t)} \!\gets\! \mathbf{w}^{(t-1)}$
			\COMMENT{skip round}
			\ENDIF
			\STATE $\mathcal{H} \!\gets\! \mathcal{H}
			\cup \{\tau_i : i \in \Sset^{(t)}\}$
			\ENDFOR
			\RETURN $\mathbf{w}^{(T)}$
		\end{algorithmic}
	\end{algorithm}

	This section presents the algorithmic components invoked by
	Algorithm~\ref{alg:async_kaas}.  Section~\ref{sec:waterfill} derives
	the optimal volume allocation for a given device set.
	Section~\ref{sec:sa_select} introduces straggler-aware device

	Section~\ref{sec:timeout} describes three timeout policies, and

	% ── 4.1 Volume Allocation via Water-Filling ──────────────
	\subsection{Volume allocation via water-filling}\label{sec:waterfill}
	
	For a fixed device subset~$\Sset$ with residual budget
	$\Bres = B - \sum_{i \in \Sset} a_i$, the volume allocation
	sub-problem seeks to maximize aggregate quality:
	\begin{equation}\label{eq:subproblem}
		\max_{\{v_i\}} \;
		\sum_{i \in \Sset} \rho_i \,\frac{v_i}{v_i + \theta_i}
		\;\;\text{s.t.}\;\;
		\sum_{i \in \Sset} b_i v_i \leq \Bres,\;
		v_i \in [0, \vmax].
	\end{equation}
	
	\begin{proposition}[Water-Filling Allocation]\label{prop:wf}
		The optimal solution to~\eqref{eq:subproblem} is
		\begin{equation}\label{eq:wf_sol}
			v_i^* = \min\!\Biggl\{
			\Biggl[\sqrt{\frac{\rho_i\,\theta_i}{\nu^*\, b_i}}
			- \theta_i \Biggr]^{\!+}\!,\; \vmax
			\Biggr\}, \;\; \forall\, i \in \Sset,
		\end{equation}
		where $[x]^+ = \max(x, 0)$ and $\nu^* > 0$ is the unique water
		level satisfying $\sum_{i \in \Sset} b_i v_i^* = \Bres$.
	\end{proposition}
	
	\begin{pf}
		The objective is strictly concave in each~$v_i$
		($\partial^2 q_i / \partial v_i^2 < 0$) and the constraint set is
		convex.  Slater's condition holds whenever $\Bres > 0$.  Writing the
		Lagrangian and setting $\partial \mathcal{L} / \partial v_i = 0$
		yields $\rho_i \theta_i / (v_i + \theta_i)^2 = \nu b_i$, which
		gives~\eqref{eq:wf_sol}.  Uniqueness of~$\nu^*$ follows from the
		intermediate value theorem, since
		$\sum_i b_i v_i^*(\nu)$ is continuous and strictly decreasing
		in~$\nu$.  The water level is computed via bisection in
		$O(|\Sset|\log(1/\delta))$ time for tolerance~$\delta$.
		\qed\end{pf}
	
	The efficiency index
	$\eta_i \triangleq \rho_i / (b_i \theta_i)$ captures the marginal
	quality gain per unit cost at zero allocation.
	From~\eqref{eq:wf_sol}, device~$i$ receives a positive allocation if
	and only if $\eta_i > \nu^*$; devices below this threshold are
	effectively excluded by the water-filling mechanism.
	
	% ── 4.2 Straggler-Aware Device Selection ─────────────────
	\subsection{Straggler-aware device selection}\label{sec:sa_select}
	
	In a synchronous setting, the ES selects devices to maximize the
	deterministic set quality
	$Q(\Sset) = \max_{\{v_i\}} \sum_{i \in \Sset} q_i(v_i)$ subject to
	budget constraint~\eqref{eq:det_budget}, solved by
	Proposition~\ref{prop:wf}.  In the asynchronous setting, however,
	each device~$i$ only delivers its complete contribution with
	probability~$\pi_i(D)$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:pi}).  The ES should therefore
	maximize the expected aggregate quality after straggler realization.
	
	Let $X_i \sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(\pi_i(D))$ denote the indicator
	that device~$i$ completes on time.  The realized quality is the
	random variable
	$Q^{\mathrm{real}}(\Sset) = \sum_{i \in \Sset} X_i \cdot
	q_i(v_i^*)$.  By linearity of expectation:
	\begin{equation}\label{eq:expected_quality}
		\mathbb{E}\bigl[Q^{\mathrm{real}}(\Sset)\bigr]
		= \sum_{i \in \Sset} \pi_i(D) \cdot q_i(v_i^*)
		\triangleq \tilde{Q}(\Sset).
	\end{equation}
	We call $\tilde{Q}(\Sset)$ the expected quality function, which
	replaces~$Q(\Sset)$ as the optimization objective in the asynchronous
	setting.  Formally, $\tilde{Q}$ is obtained by solving the modified
	allocation problem:
	\begin{subequations}\label{eq:async_problem}
		\begin{align}
			\tilde{Q}(\Sset)
			&\triangleq \max_{\{v_i\}}
			\sum_{i \in \Sset}
			\underbrace{\pi_i(D) \cdot \rho_i}_{\tilde{\rho}_i}
			\,\frac{v_i}{v_i + \theta_i}
			\label{eq:async_obj} \\
			&\;\;\text{s.t.}\;\;
			\textstyle\sum_{i \in \Sset}(a_i + b_i v_i) \leq B,\;
			v_i \in [0, \vmax].
			\label{eq:async_budget}
		\end{align}
	\end{subequations}
	This is structurally identical to~\eqref{eq:det_problem} with the
	effective fidelity $\tilde{\rho}_i = \pi_i(D) \cdot \rho_i$
	replacing~$\rho_i$.  Consequently, Proposition~\ref{prop:wf} applies
	directly with~$\tilde{\rho}_i$ in place of~$\rho_i$, and the
	straggler-aware efficiency index becomes
	$\tilde{\eta}_i = \tilde{\rho}_i / (b_i \theta_i)
	= \pi_i(D) \cdot \eta_i$.
	The preservation of structural properties follows immediately.
	
	\begin{proposition}[Submodularity under Straggler Awareness]
		\label{prop:sub_async}
		$\tilde{Q}: 2^{\Mset} \to \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ is monotone
		non-decreasing and submodular.
	\end{proposition}
	
	\begin{pf}
		For any fixed deadline~$D$, the completion
		probability~$\pi_i(D)$ is a non-negative constant associated with
		each device~$i$.  Problem~\eqref{eq:async_problem} is therefore an
		instance of~\eqref{eq:det_problem} with modified fidelity parameters
		$\tilde{\rho}_i = \pi_i(D) \cdot \rho_i \in (0, \rho_i]$.
		Monotonicity and submodularity of the parametric family
		$Q_{\rho}(\Sset) = \max_{\{v_i\}} \sum_{i \in \Sset}
		\rho_i' v_i / (v_i + \theta_i)$ subject to a knapsack constraint
		hold for any $\rho_i' > 0$: adding a device to a smaller set leaves
		more residual budget, and the concavity of each~$q_i$ implies weakly
		larger marginal gain \citep{boyd_convex}.
		\qed\end{pf}
	
	\begin{theorem}[Approximation Guarantee for $\tilde{Q}$]
		\label{thm:approx_async}
		The greedy algorithm (Algorithm~\ref{alg:dash_select}) returns
		$\Sset^{\mathrm{g}}$ satisfying
		\begin{equation}\label{eq:greedy_bound}
			\tilde{Q}(\Sset^{\mathrm{g}})
			\geq \tfrac{1}{2}(1 - 1/e) \cdot \tilde{Q}^*,
		\end{equation}
		where
		$\tilde{Q}^* = \max_{\Sset \subseteq \Mset} \tilde{Q}(\Sset)$
		is the optimal expected quality.
	\end{theorem}
	
	\begin{pf}
		$\tilde{Q}$ is monotone submodular
		(Proposition~\ref{prop:sub_async}) and the budget
		constraint~\eqref{eq:async_budget} is a knapsack constraint.  The
		result follows from the $\tfrac{1}{2}(1 - 1/e)$ approximation ratio
		for greedy maximization of monotone submodular functions under a
		knapsack constraint \citep{khuller_knapsack}.
		\qed\end{pf}
	
	We now relate the expected quality~$\tilde{Q}^*$ to the
	deterministic optimum $Q^* = \max_{\Sset} Q(\Sset)$, which represents
	the quality achievable if all devices always complete on time.
	
	\begin{theorem}[Expected Quality Bound under Straggler Rate]
		\label{thm:straggler_bound}
		Let $\pi_{\min} = \min_{i \in \Mset} \pi_i(D)$ and define the
		worst-case straggler probability $\bar{p} = 1 - \pi_{\min}$.
		Then the greedy solution satisfies
		\begin{equation}\label{eq:straggler_bound}
			\tilde{Q}(\Sset^{\mathrm{g}})
			\geq (1 - \bar{p}) \cdot
			\tfrac{1}{2}(1 - 1/e) \cdot Q^*.
		\end{equation}
	\end{theorem}
	
	\begin{pf}
		Let~$\Sset^*$ denote the optimal device set for the deterministic
		problem, with water-filling allocation $\{v_i^{\star}\}$ achieving
		$Q^* = \sum_{i \in \Sset^*} q_i(v_i^{\star})$.
		
		The allocation $\{v_i^{\star}\}$ is feasible for
		problem~\eqref{eq:async_problem} with $\Sset = \Sset^*$ (same budget
		constraint).  Therefore:
		\begin{align}
			\tilde{Q}(\Sset^*)
			&\geq \sum_{i \in \Sset^*}
			\pi_i(D) \cdot \rho_i
			\frac{v_i^{\star}}{v_i^{\star} + \theta_i}
			\notag \\
			&\geq \pi_{\min}
			\sum_{i \in \Sset^*}
			\rho_i \frac{v_i^{\star}}{v_i^{\star} + \theta_i}
			= \pi_{\min} \cdot Q^*.
			\label{eq:step1}
		\end{align}
		The first inequality uses the feasibility of~$\{v_i^{\star}\}$ (the
		$\tilde{Q}$-optimal water-filling can only yield a higher value); the
		second applies $\pi_i(D) \geq \pi_{\min}$ for all~$i$.
		Since $\tilde{Q}^* \geq \tilde{Q}(\Sset^*)$, we have
		$\tilde{Q}^* \geq \pi_{\min} \cdot Q^*$.
		Combining with Theorem~\ref{thm:approx_async}:
		\begin{align}
			\tilde{Q}(\Sset^{\mathrm{g}})
			&\geq \tfrac{1}{2}(1 - 1/e) \cdot \tilde{Q}^*
			\notag \\
			&\geq (1 - \bar{p}) \cdot
			\tfrac{1}{2}(1 - 1/e) \cdot Q^*.
			\label{eq:step2}
		\end{align}
		\qed\end{pf}
	
	The bound~\eqref{eq:straggler_bound} quantifies the price of
	asynchrony: the greedy expected quality degrades by at most a factor
	of $(1 - \bar{p})$ relative to the deterministic optimum.  This is a
	worst-case bound based on~$\pi_{\min}$; in practice, the
	straggler-aware selection preferentially includes reliable devices
	(high~$\pi_i$), so the effective average completion probability
	across~$\Sset^{\mathrm{g}}$ is substantially higher
	than~$\pi_{\min}$.  The modified efficiency index
	$\tilde{\eta}_i = \pi_i \cdot \rho_i / (b_i \theta_i)$ naturally
	balances reliability against knowledge quality: a device with
	excellent data quality ($\rho_i$ high, $\theta_i$ low) but poor
	connectivity ($\pi_i$ low) is down-ranked relative to a moderately
	informative but reliable peer.
	
	Algorithm~\ref{alg:dash_select} differs from a standard
	deterministic greedy scheduler in exactly one line: the marginal gain
	computation multiplies by~$\pi_i(D)$.  All other steps---ranking,
	water-filling, greedy loop---remain identical.
	
	\begin{algorithm}[t]
		\caption{DASH-Select: Straggler-Aware Scheduling}
		\label{alg:dash_select}
		\begin{algorithmic}[1]
			\REQUIRE Device pool $\Mset$, budget $B$, deadline $D$,
			profiles $\{(\rho_i, \theta_i, a_i, b_i)\}$,
			straggler model $(\mu_n, \sigma_n)$, tolerance $\delta$
			\ENSURE Selected set $\Sset$, allocations $\{v_i^*\}$
			\STATE // Compute straggler-aware efficiency
			\FOR{each $i \in \Mset$}
			\STATE $\pi_i \gets \Pr[\tau_i \leq D]$
			via Eq.~\eqref{eq:pi}
			\STATE $\tilde{\eta}_i \gets
			\pi_i \cdot \rho_i / (b_i \theta_i)$
			\ENDFOR
			\STATE Sort devices by $\tilde{\eta}_i$ (decreasing)
			\STATE // Greedy selection on $\tilde{Q}$
			\STATE $\Sset \gets \emptyset$
			\FOR{each device $i$ in sorted order}
			\IF{$a_i + \sum_{j \in \Sset} a_j > B$}
			\STATE \textbf{continue}
			\ENDIF
			\STATE $\Sset' \gets \Sset \cup \{i\}$
			\STATE $\{v_j\} \!\gets\!
			\mathrm{WaterFill}(\Sset',
			B {-} \textstyle\sum_{j \in \Sset'}\! a_j,
			\{\tilde{\rho}_j\}, \delta)$
			\IF{$\tilde{Q}(\Sset') > \tilde{Q}(\Sset)$}
			\STATE $\Sset \gets \Sset'$
			\ENDIF
			\ENDFOR
			\STATE // Final allocation
			\STATE $\{v_i^*\}_{i \in \Sset} \!\gets\!
			\mathrm{WaterFill}(\Sset,
			B {-} \textstyle\sum_{i \in \Sset}\! a_i,
			\{\tilde{\rho}_i\}, \delta)$
			\RETURN $\Sset,\; \{v_i^*\}$
		\end{algorithmic}
	\end{algorithm}
	
	% ── 4.3 Timeout Policy Design ────────────────────────────
	\subsection{Timeout policy design}\label{sec:timeout}
	
	The deadline~$D^{(t)}$ controls the trade-off between round latency
	(shorter deadline leads to faster rounds) and knowledge utilization
	(longer deadline allows more devices to complete, yielding higher
	per-round quality).  We consider three policies with increasing
	adaptiveness.
	
	The simplest strategy, which we call the \emph{fixed-deadline} policy,
	sets $D^{(t)} = D_0$ for all rounds~$t$, where $D_0$ is a
	system-level constant chosen by the operator.  This provides
	predictable per-round wall-clock time and is straightforward to
	implement, but cannot adapt to changing network conditions.  Setting
	$D_0$ too low wastes scheduled resources through excessive timeouts,
	while setting it too high approaches synchronous behavior.
	
	The \emph{adaptive-deadline} policy adjusts the deadline based on
	observed latency statistics from the previous round.  Let
	$\{\tau_i^{(t-1)}\}$ denote the latencies observed in round~$t{-}1$.
	The adaptive policy sets:
	\begin{equation}\label{eq:adaptive}
		D^{(t)} = \max\!\Bigl(D_{\min},\;\;
		\alpha_{\mathrm{ema}} \,
		\mathrm{Pctl}_p\!\bigl(\{\tau_i^{(t-1)}\}\bigr)
		+ (1 {-} \alpha_{\mathrm{ema}}) \, D^{(t-1)}\Bigr),
	\end{equation}
	where $\mathrm{Pctl}_p$ is the $p$-th percentile of the empirical
	latency distribution, $\alpha_{\mathrm{ema}} \in (0,1)$ is an
	exponential moving average smoothing coefficient that prevents
	oscillation, and $D_{\min} = \beta \cdot D_{\mathrm{warmup}}$ is a
	hard lower bound that prevents the deadline from collapsing.  During a
	warmup phase of~$W$ rounds, the policy falls back to a warmup
	deadline~$D_{\mathrm{warmup}}$ estimated from device profiles.  The
	percentile parameter~$p$ directly controls the target completion rate.
	
	The $D_{\min}$ floor is a critical safeguard against a
	\emph{deadline spiral}: without it, a tight deadline reduces the
	volume of logits each device can upload, which shortens observed
	latencies, which in turn drives the adaptive deadline even lower in
	subsequent rounds.  This positive feedback loop can cause the deadline
	to converge to a value so small that no device contributes meaningful
	knowledge.  The floor~$D_{\min}$ breaks this loop by guaranteeing a
	minimum collection window regardless of the latency history.  The
	effectiveness of this mechanism is validated empirically in
	Section~\ref{sec:eval}.
	
	The \emph{partial-accept} policy uses the same adaptive deadline
	mechanism, but additionally accepts partial logit uploads from devices
	in the Partial outcome category (Definition~\ref{def:outcome}).
	Partial contributions enter the aggregation with reduced quality
	weight since $q_i(v_i^{\mathrm{recv}}) < q_i(v_i^*)$ whenever
	$v_i^{\mathrm{recv}} < v_i^*$.  This policy maximizes knowledge
	utilization by retaining computation that the device has already
	performed, at the cost of slightly noisier aggregation from
	incomplete logit sets.
	
	% ── 4.4 Quality-Weighted Aggregation ─────────────────────
	\subsection{Quality-weighted aggregation}\label{sec:aggregation}
	
	After the deadline expires, the ES aggregates logits from the
	set of responding devices~$\Srecv$, which includes all devices with
	Complete outcomes and, under the partial-accept policy, those with
	Partial outcomes as well.  Each device $i \in \Srecv$ has delivered
	$v_i^{\mathrm{recv}}$ logit vectors (equal to~$v_i^*$ for complete
	devices, or given by~\eqref{eq:partial} for partial devices).  The
	aggregation weight for device~$i$ is
	\begin{equation}\label{eq:agg_weight}
		w_i = \frac{\rho_i \cdot q_i(v_i^{\mathrm{recv}})}
		{\displaystyle\sum_{j \in \Srecv}
			\rho_j \cdot q_j(v_j^{\mathrm{recv}})},
	\end{equation}
	where $q_i(v_i^{\mathrm{recv}}) =
	\rho_i \, v_i^{\mathrm{recv}} / (v_i^{\mathrm{recv}} + \theta_i)$.
	Devices with higher privacy fidelity and more complete uploads
	receive proportionally larger weight, while partial contributions are
	naturally down-weighted through the saturation function.  The
	aggregated logit vector
	$\bar{\mathbf{z}}^{(t)} = \sum_{i \in \Srecv}
	w_i \tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i$
	is then used to update the global model via KL-divergence
	minimization:
	\begin{equation}\label{eq:kl}
		\mathbf{w}^{(t)} \!=\! \mathbf{w}^{(t-1)}
		\!-\! \alpha \nabla_{\mathbf{w}}
		\mathrm{KL}\!\bigl(
		\sigma(\bar{\mathbf{z}}^{(t)} / \tau)
		\,\|\,
		\sigma(\mathbf{w}^{(t-1)}\!(\Dref) / \tau)
		\bigr),
	\end{equation}
	where $\sigma$ is the softmax function, $\tau$ is the distillation
	temperature, and~$\alpha$ is the learning rate.
	
	In the synchronous protocol, $v_i^{\mathrm{recv}} = v_i^*$ for all
	selected devices and $\Srecv = \Sset^{(t)}$, so the aggregation
	weight reduces to $w_i \propto \rho_i \cdot q_i(v_i^*)$, recovering
	the deterministic quality weighting.  In the asynchronous setting,
	partial devices contribute with $v_i^{\mathrm{recv}} < v_i^*$,
	resulting in lower~$q_i$ and hence lower weight---a graceful
	degradation mechanism that requires no additional tuning parameters.

	\section{Performance Evaluation}\label{sec:eval}
	% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
	
	This section evaluates DASH through six groups of experiments.
	Section~\ref{sec:setup} describes the datasets, model architecture,
	baselines, and parameter settings.
	Section~\ref{sec:main_compare} presents the main comparison between
	synchronous and asynchronous methods on CIFAR-100.
	Section~\ref{sec:straggler_sweep} sweeps the straggler severity
	parameter to assess robustness.
	Section~\ref{sec:policy_exp} compares timeout policies and
	demonstrates the necessity of the $D_{\min}$ floor.
	Section~\ref{sec:scalability} scales the device pool from 20 to 200.
	Section~\ref{sec:emnist} validates the conclusions on EMNIST-ByClass,
	and Section~\ref{sec:privacy_exp} examines the interaction between
	privacy and asynchronous scheduling.
	
	% ── 5.1 Experimental Setup ───────────────────────────────
	\subsection{Experimental setup}\label{sec:setup}
	
	We evaluate DASH on two image classification benchmarks with
	non-IID data partitions.  CIFAR-100 contains 60,000 color images
	across 100 classes; we distribute the training set among devices
	using a Dirichlet allocation with concentration $\alpha = 0.3$ to
	create label-skewed local datasets.  EMNIST-ByClass contains
	approximately 697,000 handwritten character images across 62 classes
	(digits, uppercase and lowercase letters); we partition the data by
	writer identity, which produces a naturally non-IID distribution
	without synthetic manipulation.  In both cases, a public reference
	dataset~$\Dref$ of 5,000 samples is held out at the server for logit
	computation and distillation.
	
	Each device runs a lightweight CNN with three convolutional layers
	followed by batch normalization and two fully connected layers.
	Local training uses SGD with learning rate 0.01 for $E = 5$ local
	epochs per round.  Distillation uses temperature $\tau = 3.0$,
	KL-divergence weight $\alpha_{\mathrm{KD}} = 0.5$, and a maximum
	upload volume of $\vmax = 100$ logit vectors per device.  The
	straggler latency noise follows
	$\tau_i^{\mathrm{noise}} \sim \mathrm{LogNormal}(0, \sigma_n)$,
	where the severity parameter $\sigma_n$ is varied across
	experiments.  Devices have heterogeneous privacy levels drawn from a
	pool with $\rho_i \in \{1.0, 0.8, 0.5, 0.2, 0.05\}$ at proportions
	15\%, 20\%, 30\%, 20\%, and 15\%, respectively, yielding an average
	$\bar{\rho} \approx 0.51$.  The default configuration uses $M = 50$
	devices, budget $B = 50$, $T = 50$ rounds, and $\sigma_n = 0.5$.
	
	For the adaptive deadline policy, the warmup
	deadline~$D_{\mathrm{warmup}}$ is estimated from device profiles
	using the budget-constrained expected latency with a safety factor.
	The EMA coefficient is $\alpha_{\mathrm{ema}} = 0.3$, the percentile
	is $p = 0.85$, the warmup phase lasts $W = 3$ rounds, and the
	deadline floor is $D_{\min} = 0.3 \cdot D_{\mathrm{warmup}}$.  All
	results are averaged over three independent runs with seeds 42, 123,
	and 456, and reported as mean $\pm$ standard deviation.  Experiments
	are conducted on an NVIDIA RTX 5070 Ti GPU with 16\,GB VRAM.
	
	We compare DASH against five baselines that span the synchronous--asynchronous
	spectrum.  Sync-Greedy is the synchronous variant of DASH that sets
	$\pi_i = 1$ for all devices (equivalently,
	$\tilde{\rho}_i = \rho_i$) and waits for all selected devices to
	finish; it serves as a natural ablation baseline that isolates the
	effect of asynchronous scheduling.  Sync-Full selects all $M$
	devices synchronously with no budget constraint, representing the
	worst-case synchronous protocol.  FedBuff-FD adapts the buffered
	asynchronous aggregation of \citet{nguyen_fedbuff_2022} to the FD
	setting, triggering one aggregation step after collecting responses
	from a fixed buffer of devices.  Random-Async selects devices
	uniformly at random and enforces a fixed deadline.  Full-Async
	selects all devices with adaptive timeout but without the
	budget-constrained scheduling of DASH.  For synchronous baselines,
	the per-round wall-clock time is set to
	$\max_{i \in \Sset^{(t)}} \tau_i$, reflecting the blocking nature
	of synchronous aggregation.
	
	% ── 5.2 Main Comparison ─────────────────────────────────
	\subsection{Main comparison}\label{sec:main_compare}
	
	We first examine the core question of whether asynchronous scheduling
	can match synchronous per-round learning quality while reducing
	wall-clock convergence time.  Fig.~\ref{fig:acc_round} plots test
	accuracy against communication round for all six methods under the
	default setting ($M = 50$, $\sigma_n = 0.5$).  DASH and Sync-Greedy
	follow nearly identical trajectories throughout training, both
	reaching approximately 44\% after 50 rounds, which confirms that
	incorporating completion probabilities into the scheduling objective
	does not degrade per-round distillation quality.  Sync-Full converges
	at the same rate as these two methods, verifying that the
	budget-constrained selection retains sufficient knowledge diversity.
	Full-Async trails by roughly 9 percentage points, indicating that
	selecting all devices without budget-aware allocation dilutes the
	aggregated logit quality.  Random-Async and FedBuff-FD remain below
	24\% and 18\%, respectively, demonstrating that neither random
	selection nor buffered aggregation transfers effectively to the
	logit-based distillation setting.

	% ── 5.3 Straggler Severity Sweep ─────────────────────────
	\subsection{Straggler severity sweep}\label{sec:straggler_sweep}
	
	To assess robustness, we vary the straggler severity parameter
	$\sigma_n \in \{0.0, 0.3, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5\}$ while keeping all other
	settings at default.  Fig.~\ref{fig:straggler_acc} shows that DASH
	maintains stable accuracy across the entire range, declining by only
	1.2 percentage points from $\sigma_n = 0$ (44.68\%) to
	$\sigma_n = 1.5$ (43.47\%).  Sync-Greedy exhibits similarly stable
	accuracy because it waits for all devices regardless of latency, but
	this stability comes at increasing wall-clock cost.
	
	Fig.~\ref{fig:straggler_wc} reveals the key distinction.
	Sync-Greedy's total wall-clock time grows from 3,073\,s at
	$\sigma_n = 0$ to 3,551\,s at $\sigma_n = 1.5$, a 15.6\% increase
	driven by the tail latencies of the slowest devices.  DASH absorbs
	the increased straggler severity with only a 13.4\% wall-clock
	increase (977\,s to 1,108\,s), and the relative speedup
	over Sync-Greedy grows monotonically from $3.15\times$ to
	$3.20\times$.  This widening gap confirms the theoretical intuition
	behind Theorem~\ref{thm:straggler_bound}: as straggler severity
	increases, the completion probability~$\pi_i(D)$ decreases for
	unreliable devices, and the straggler-aware efficiency index
	$\tilde{\eta}_i$ automatically down-ranks them in favor of more
	reliable peers.  FedBuff-FD's accuracy remains flat near 17\%
	regardless of $\sigma_n$, further confirming that its buffered
	aggregation mechanism does not benefit from reduced straggler noise.

	\subsection{Timeout policy comparison}\label{sec:policy_exp}
	
	We compare six timeout configurations---three fixed-deadline settings
	($D_0 \in \{5, 10, 20\}$\,s) and three adaptive-deadline settings
	($p \in \{0.5, 0.7, 0.9\}$)---each tested with and without the
	$D_{\min}$ floor, yielding twelve configurations in total.
	Fig.~\ref{fig:policy_scatter} plots the final accuracy against wall-clock
	time for each configuration, where circles denote configurations with
	$D_{\min}$ and crosses denote those without.
	
	Several observations emerge.  First, the $D_{\min}$ floor
	consistently improves accuracy across all configurations, with the
	largest gain appearing for the most aggressive settings: fixed
	$D_0 = 5$\,s without $D_{\min}$ collapses to 35.96\%, while adding
	$D_{\min}$ recovers 7.58 percentage points.  Second, the
	adaptive policy with $p = 0.7$ and $D_{\min}$ achieves the best
	accuracy--time trade-off (45.05\% at 871\,s), dominating all other
	configurations on the Pareto frontier.  Third, high-percentile
	adaptive settings ($p = 0.9$) approach synchronous wall-clock times
	without meaningful accuracy gains, confirming that overly generous
	deadlines waste time waiting for marginal contributions.
	
	Fig.~\ref{fig:policy_deadline} visualises the deadline evolution across
	rounds for the three adaptive settings.  Without $D_{\min}$, the
	$p = 0.5$ configuration exhibits the deadline spiral described in
	Section~\ref{sec:timeout}: the deadline drops rapidly from the
	warmup value and continues to decrease throughout training, falling
	below 10\,s by round~20.  The $D_{\min}$ floor halts this descent at
	approximately 16\,s, preserving a sufficient collection window for
	meaningful logit uploads.  The $p = 0.9$ setting converges to a
	higher deadline regardless of~$D_{\min}$, since the 90th percentile
	of observed latencies is already large.

	\subsection{Scalability}\label{sec:scalability}
	
	We scale the device pool from $M = 20$ to $M = 200$ with a
	proportional budget $B = 2.5M$ and 50 rounds per experiment.
	Fig.~\ref{fig:scale_acc} shows that both DASH and Sync-Greedy
	benefit from larger pools, with accuracy increasing from
	approximately 45\% at $M = 20$ to 47--48\% at $M = 100$ before
	saturating.  DASH consistently outperforms Sync-Greedy by 0.1 to
	1.1 percentage points across all pool sizes.  FedBuff-FD remains
	near 17\% regardless of~$M$, since its fixed buffer size does not
	exploit the growing device diversity.

	striking divergence.  Sync-Greedy's total time grows from 6,952\,s

	increases with pool size.  DASH's wall-clock time remains nearly
	flat across all scales (2,494\,s to 2,889\,s), since the adaptive
	deadline and straggler-aware selection together absorb the growing
	tail-latency risk.  The resulting speedup increases monotonically
	from $2.79\times$ at $M = 20$ to $3.49\times$ at $M = 200$,
	confirming that DASH's advantage grows with system scale---precisely
	the regime where synchronous protocols become most impractical.

	\subsection{Privacy robustness under asynchronous operation}\label{sec:privacy_exp}
	
	The privacy degradation factor~$\rho_i$ controls the noise level
	applied to uploaded logits, with lower values providing stronger
	privacy at the cost of reduced knowledge fidelity.  To isolate the
	interaction between privacy and asynchronous scheduling, we run DASH
	under four uniform privacy settings: $\tilde{\rho} \approx 0.98$
	(no privacy), $0.80$ (mild), $0.55$ (moderate), and $0.10$ (strong).
	
	Fig.~\ref{fig:privacy_acc} shows that best accuracy decreases
	monotonically from 49.06\% to 39.60\% as privacy strengthens, which
	is expected since heavier noise degrades the information content of
	the distilled logits.  The dashed line overlaying the same panel
	reports wall-clock time, which remains essentially constant near
	916\,s across all privacy levels.  This decoupling is a desirable
	property: the scheduling and deadline mechanisms operate on latency
	and budget parameters that are independent of the noise level, so
	strengthening privacy does not interfere with the system's ability
	to handle stragglers.
	
	Fig.~\ref{fig:privacy_conv} plots the per-round convergence curves
	for the four settings.  All configurations follow smooth trajectories
	with no instability, and the gap between privacy levels remains
	roughly constant throughout training, indicating that the
	quality-weighted aggregation~\eqref{eq:agg_weight} naturally adapts
	to noisier inputs without requiring privacy-specific tuning.
	
	In the main experiment (Table~\ref{tab:main}), devices have
	heterogeneous privacy levels with an average
	$\bar{\rho} \approx 0.51$.  DASH achieves 44.47\% under this
	heterogeneous setting, which is slightly higher than the 42.96\%
	observed under uniform $\tilde{\rho} \approx 0.55$ in the privacy
	sweep.  This difference arises because heterogeneous privacy allows
	a few low-noise devices ($\rho_i = 1.0$) to contribute high-fidelity
	logits that disproportionately benefit the distillation quality,
	an effect that is averaged out under uniform privacy.

	\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discuss}
	% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
	
	This section discusses practical guidelines derived from the
	experimental results, the relationship to the conference version of
	this work, and limitations that suggest directions for future
	research.
	
	% ── 6.1 When to Use Sync vs Async ────────────────────────
	\subsection{Synchronous versus asynchronous operation}\label{sec:sync_vs_async}
	
	The experiments reveal that DASH and Sync-Greedy achieve nearly
	identical per-round accuracy (Section~\ref{sec:main_compare}),
	meaning that the straggler-aware scheduling does not sacrifice
	distillation quality.  The advantage of DASH is purely in wall-clock
	efficiency, and the magnitude of this advantage depends on two
	factors: the straggler severity~$\sigma_n$ and the pool size~$M$.
	When $\sigma_n$ is close to zero and $M$ is small, the latency
	distribution is narrow and the synchronous round time is already
	short; in this regime the overhead of maintaining deadline estimates
	and latency histories may not be justified.  As $\sigma_n$ or $M$
	increases, however, the synchronous round time grows rapidly due to
	tail latencies, while DASH's wall-clock time remains nearly flat
	(Sections~\ref{sec:straggler_sweep} and~\ref{sec:scalability}).  As
	a practical guideline, DASH is recommended whenever the device pool
	is heterogeneous in computational capability or network quality, or
	when the pool size exceeds a few dozen devices.  For homogeneous
	deployments with stable network conditions, Sync-Greedy offers a
	simpler alternative with comparable accuracy.
	
	% ── 6.2 Practical Deadline Tuning ────────────────────────
	\subsection{Practical deadline tuning}\label{sec:deadline_tuning}
	
	The timeout policy comparison (Section~\ref{sec:policy_exp})
	provides three actionable insights for system operators.  First, the
	adaptive policy consistently outperforms fixed deadlines across a
	wide range of percentile settings, since it tracks the evolving
	latency distribution without manual re-tuning.  Second, the
	$D_{\min}$ floor is not merely a safety net but a structural
	necessity: without it, the adaptive policy is susceptible to a
	deadline spiral that can collapse per-round quality by over 7
	percentage points in the most aggressive configurations.  Third, the
	percentile parameter~$p$ offers a smooth knob for trading off
	accuracy against round latency, with values in the range
	$[0.7,\, 0.9]$ producing robust results across all tested settings.
	The warmup phase of~$W$ rounds is short (three rounds in our
	experiments) and primarily serves to bootstrap the latency history
	before adaptive estimation begins; its length is not a sensitive
	parameter.
	
	% ── 6.3 Relationship to Conference Version ───────────────
	\subsection{Relationship to conference version}\label{sec:relationship}
	
	This paper extends our conference work\footnote{Under review at IEEE International Conference on Edge Computing and Communications (EDGE), 2026.} on resource-aware distillation
	scheduling for synchronous edge networks.  The conference version
	formulates device scheduling as budgeted submodular maximization
	under a deterministic quality model, derives the water-filling
	allocation and greedy selection algorithm, and evaluates on CIFAR-100
	with a single synchronous protocol.  The present paper makes the
	following extensions: (i)~an asynchronous protocol with a
	three-outcome straggler model (complete, partial, timeout) driven by
	log-normal latency distributions; (ii)~straggler-aware device
	selection that integrates completion probabilities into the
	submodular objective, with a new expected quality bound
	(Theorem~\ref{thm:straggler_bound}); (iii)~three timeout policies
	with the $D_{\min}$ floor mechanism; and (iv)~comprehensive
	experiments across six experiment groups covering straggler severity,
	policy ablation, scalability to 200 devices, cross-dataset validation
	on EMNIST, and privacy robustness.  Approximately 65\% of the content
	is new.
	
	% ── 6.4 Limitations and Future Work ──────────────────────
	\subsection{Limitations and future work}\label{sec:limitations}
	
	Several limitations of the current work suggest promising directions
	for future research.  First, the straggler latency model is
	evaluated through simulation rather than on a physical testbed with
	real network conditions and heterogeneous edge devices.  While the
	log-normal noise model is well-established in the networking
	literature, deploying DASH on a real-world platform would reveal
	practical considerations such as bursty interference patterns,
	device mobility, and OS-level scheduling jitter that are not captured
	by i.i.d.\ noise assumptions.
	
	Second, DASH adopts a round-level asynchronous design in which all
	logits are computed from the current global model, resulting in zero
	staleness.  This is a deliberate simplification that keeps the
	aggregation semantics clean.  A more aggressive pipeline-level
	asynchronous design, where devices may compute logits based on
	different versions of the global model, could further reduce idle
	time but would require staleness correction mechanisms tailored to
	logit-based distillation.  Designing such mechanisms with provable
	convergence guarantees is a non-trivial extension that we leave to
	future work.
	
	Third, the current framework assumes that all devices share the
	same model architecture.  Extending DASH to heterogeneous model
	architectures, where different devices run models of varying depth
	or width, would broaden its applicability to realistic edge
	deployments.  The quality function and aggregation weights would
	need to account for architecture-dependent logit distributions, an
	aspect that connects to the broader literature on heterogeneous
	federated distillation.
	
	Finally, while Theorem~\ref{thm:straggler_bound} provides a
	per-round expected quality bound, a formal convergence rate analysis
	that accounts for the stochastic variation in per-round quality
	across the full training trajectory remains an open problem.  Such
	an analysis would provide stronger end-to-end guarantees on the
	final model accuracy as a function of the straggler rate, budget,
	and number of rounds.
	
	
	% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
	\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclude}
	% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
	
	This paper presented DASH, a deadline-aware scheduling framework
	for asynchronous federated distillation over heterogeneous edge
	networks.  DASH introduces a three-outcome straggler model
	(complete, partial, timeout) driven by log-normal latency
	distributions and integrates device completion probabilities into a
	submodular greedy selection algorithm.  The resulting straggler-aware
	efficiency index preserves submodularity, enabling a greedy
	scheduler with a provable
	$(1-\bar{p})\tfrac{1}{2}(1-1/e)$ expected quality guarantee relative
	to the deterministic optimum.  Three timeout policies---fixed,
	adaptive, and partial-accept---offer system-level trade-offs between
	round latency and knowledge utilization, with the adaptive policy
	augmented by a $D_{\min}$ floor to prevent deadline spiral.
	
	Experiments on CIFAR-100 and EMNIST-ByClass (Extended MNIST) with up to 200 devices
	demonstrate that DASH achieves accuracy comparable to synchronous
	scheduling while reducing wall-clock convergence time by
	$2$--$3.5\times$, with the speedup increasing monotonically as the
	device pool scales.  The framework maintains stable accuracy across
	a wide range of straggler severities and privacy levels, confirming
	that the scheduling and aggregation mechanisms are robust to both
	latency and noise variations.  These results establish that
	quality-aware asynchronous scheduling is both feasible and effective
	for federated distillation at the network edge, opening a path
	toward practical deployment of logit-based collaborative learning
	in large-scale heterogeneous IoT environments.
